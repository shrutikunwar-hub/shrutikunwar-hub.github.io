01 / Problem Statement

The Question

Amazon Prime bundles delivery, streaming, music, and storage into a single subscription — but consumers don't value every feature equally. As Amazon considers splitting or repositioning its membership tiers, understanding which attributes truly drive consumer preference becomes critical for pricing strategy.

This project used choice-based conjoint analysis to answer: How do delivery speed, video ad experience, price, and additional benefits trade off against each other in consumer decision-making? And which tier configuration wins in a simulated market?

02 / Methodology

The Approach

A three-stage design combining fractional factorial survey construction, conditional logit estimation, and market share simulation.

01

Survey Design

14 fractional factorial product profiles generating 91 unique pairwise choice sets, deployed via Qualtrics to Masters and undergraduate students.

02

Conjoint Estimation

Merged respondent choices with attribute profiles; constructed dummy variables; estimated a conditional logit model using statsmodels to extract part-worth utilities.

03

Market Simulation

Converted utilities to dollar values via price anchoring, computed attribute importance via the range method, and simulated market shares across four product scenarios.

03 / Study Design

Attributes & Levels

05 / Key Findings

What the Data Revealed

Price is the Dominant Driver

Highest Attribute Importance · 44.1%

Price commanded the largest utility range across all attributes. Dropping from $14.49 to $6.49 produced a substantial positive part-worth (+0.74), confirming that consumers are highly price-sensitive when choosing between Amazon membership tiers.

Ad-Free Video Matters More Than Expected

Negative Part-Worth for Ads · −0.32

The baseline (ad-free Prime Video) was significantly preferred over the limited-ads variant. Consumers assigned a −$3.47 dollar penalty to accepting ads — indicating that video experience quality is a strong retention driver and should not be compromised lightly.

Unlimited Delivery Has Hidden Value

Negative Part-Worth for Restriction · −0.42

Free delivery on all items (baseline) was preferred over the $35-threshold variant, which consumers penalized by −$4.54. The restriction to orders above $35 introduced the single largest non-price disutility in the model.

Additional Benefits Show Modest Impact

Lowest Attribute Importance · 12.2%

Prime Music (+0.22) and Amazon Photo Storage (+0.14) both showed positive part-worths relative to no added benefits, but importance ranked lowest overall. Consumers value these perks as tie-breakers, not primary decision drivers.

07 / Recommendations

What to Do About It

The conjoint results point to a clear price-led segmentation opportunity — with quality thresholds that Amazon should not cross even to capture budget subscribers.

R.01

Anchor the Entry Tier at $6.49

Price sensitivity is highest in this range. A $6.49 entry tier captures the broadest share even with delivery and video tradeoffs — making it the most effective acquisition lever.

R.02

Protect Ad-Free Video as a Premium Signal

Consumers penalize ad-supported video by −$3.47. Reserve ad-free streaming as a premium differentiator — introducing it at mid-tier ($7.49) dramatically improves perceived value.

R.03

Justify Premium Pricing With Unlimited Delivery

Unconditional free delivery is the strongest non-price driver. The $14.49 tier should always include it — without it, consumers see no rational reason to pay full price.

R.04

Bundle Add-ons as Retention, Not Acquisition

Music and Photo Storage add utility but don't win new subscribers. Deploy them as loyalty perks for existing members rather than as primary acquisition hooks.
